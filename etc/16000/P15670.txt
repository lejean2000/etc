<p class="preamble-title">
POINTS ON THE EULER LINE, BY COMBOS: X(15670)-X(15723)
</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">
</header>
<div class="preamble-sect">

<p>
Certain points on the Euler line are easily represented as combos, as defined in the Introduction, in Part 1.  If P and Q are points on the Euler line, then the points h P + k Q, where h and k are constants, comprise the <b>thinline</b> of P and Q, denoted by TL(P,Q).  Points X(15670)-X(15680) are on TL(X(2),X(21)), and points X(15681)-X(15723), X(15759), X(15764), X(15765) are on TL(X(2), X(3)).
</p>
<p>
The Euler line contains infinitely many thinlines, all of which contain the point X(2)-X(3) at infinity.  Otherwise, distinct thinlines meet in at most one point.
</p>
<p>
In general, if P and Q are points, then many combos h P + k Q are composites of the midpoint and reflection operations.  Let m = midpoint and r = reflection.  Then
</p>
<p>
P + Q = m(P,Q)<br>
P - 2 Q = r(P,Q)<br>
3 P + Q = m(P,m(P,Q))<br>
3 P - Q = m(r(Q,P)),P)<br>
3 P - 2 Q = r(r(P,Q)),P)<br>
3 P - 4 Q = r(P,r(P,Q))<br>
4 P - 5 Q = r(r(Q,P),r(P,Q))<br>
5 P + 3 Q = m(m(m(P,Q),r(Q,P)),Q)<br>
</p>
<p>
An open question is whether h*P+k*Q is such a composite for all choices h, k or integers (not both 0).  Note that TL(P,Q) also contains points such as P + 2<sup>1/2</sup>*Q which is not such a composite.
</p>
<p>
The appearance of (h,k,j) in the following lists (Groups 1 to 9) means that the point h*X(2) + k*X(3) = X(j), on TL(X(2),X(3)):
</p>

</p>
<p>
X(1113) = 3 X(2) + (-3 + J) X(3) = (- 1 + J) X(3) + X(4)<br>
X(1114) = 3 X(2) - (3 + J) X(3) = - (1 + J) X(3) + X(4)<br>

X(4) = (1 + J) X(1113) + (1 - J) X(1114)<br>
X(5) = (2 + J) X(1113) + (2 - J) X(1114)<br>
X(20) = (1 - J) X(1113) + (1 + J) X(1114)
</p>
<p>
In general, h X(2) + k X(3) = ((3 + J) h + 3 k) X(1113) + ((3 - J) h + 3 k) X(1114).  Also,<br>
X(21) = (p + a b c (3 + J)) X(1113) + (p + a b c (3 - J)) X(1114), where p = (a + b - c) (a - b + c) (-a + b + c).
</p>
