</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">

<div class="preamble-sect">
<p>
This preamble and centers X(14782)-X(14806) were contributed by CÃ©sar Eliud Lozada, October 10, 2017.
</p>
<p>
"When several pairs of points A, A'; B, B' ; C, C&prime; ; . . .  lying on a straight line are such that their distances from a fixed point O are connected by the relations
</p>
<p>
OA .OA' = OB.OB' = OC.OC&prime; = . . . ,
</p>
<p>
the points are called a <b>range in involution</b>." (Lachlan, R.: <i>An Elementary Treatise on Modern Pure Geometry.</i> MacMillan &amp; Co., 1893, chapter 5, pp. 37-41).
</p>
<p>
A range in involution is denoted by {A, A&prime;}, {B, B&prime;}, {C, C&prime;}. The point O, collinear with the given points, is called the <b>center of involution</b> and each pair of corresponding points, as A and A&prime;, are said to be a <b>conjugate-couple</b>.
</p>
<p>
To construct O, let {A, A'}, {B, B'} be two pairs of points on a straight line. Through A and B draw any two lines AP, BP intersecting in P and through A', B' draw A'Q, B'Q parallel to BP, AP respectively, meeting in Q. PQ meets AB in O. (Proof in the above reference). From this construction, it is clear that two pairs of points are sufficient for determining the range unambigously and its notation can be shortened to {A, A'}, {B, B'}.
</p>
<p>
<b>Some properties:</b>
</p>
<p>
&emsp;1) Suppose A, A&prime;, B, B&prime; are four distinct collinear points. Let &Gamma;<sub>A</sub> be any circle through A and A&prime; and &Gamma;<sub>B</sub> any circle through B and B&prime;. Then, for all &Gamma;<sub>A</sub> and &Gamma;<sub>B</sub>, their radical axis passes through O, the center of involution of {A, A&prime;}, {B, B&prime;}.
<br>
<br>
&emsp;2) If all the points in a range in involution lie on the same side of the center of involution, there exist two real points, one on either side of the center, each of which coincides with its own conjugate. These points are called the <b>double points of the involution</b>. (Construction: from the center of involution O draw the tangent OT to any circle through a pair of conjugate points, for example A, A&prime;. The circle (O,T) cuts AA&prime; in S and S&prime; which are the double points of the involution).
<br>
<br>
&emsp;3) Any pair of conjugate points of a range in involution are harmonic conjugates with respect to the double points of the involution.
</p>
<p>
<b>Trilinear coordinates:</b>
</p>
<p>
Supose Y = U<sub>y</sub> : V<sub>y</sub> : W<sub>y</sub>  and Z = U<sub>z</sub> : V<sub>z</sub> : W<sub>z</sub> (trilinears) and let&prime;s assume that Y&prime;, Z&prime; are such that YY' = &lambda; YZ and ZZ' = &mu; YZ. Let X be another point such that YX = &varkappa; YZ.
<br>
<br>
The center of involution of {Y, Y'}, {Z, Z'} has coordinates:
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;O = U<sub>y</sub> (&lambda; - 1) (a U<sub>z</sub> + b V<sub>z</sub> + c W<sub>z</sub>) - U<sub>z</sub> &mu; (a U<sub>y</sub> + b V<sub>y</sub> + c W<sub>y</sub>) : :
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; = (&lambda; - 1) Y - &mu; Z
</p>
<p>
If O = U<sub>o</sub> : V<sub>o</sub> : W<sub>o</sub> then X', the conjugate-couple of X, has coordinates:
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;X' = &mu; ((a U<sub>z</sub> + b V<sub>z</sub> + c W<sub>z</sub>) U<sub>y</sub> - ((U<sub>y</sub> V<sub>z</sub> - U<sub>z</sub> V<sub>y</sub>) b + (U<sub>y</sub> W<sub>z</sub> - U<sub>z</sub> W<sub>y</sub>) c) &lambda;) + &varkappa; U<sub>o</sub> : :
</p>
<p>
The double points of the involution have coordinates:
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;S<sub>&plusmn;</sub> = &plusmn; &eta; ((U<sub>y</sub> V<sub>z</sub> - U<sub>z</sub> V<sub>y</sub>) b + (U<sub>y</sub> W<sub>z</sub> - U<sub>z</sub> W<sub>y</sub>) c) &mu; + U<sub>z</sub> &mu;  (a U<sub>y</sub> + b V<sub>y</sub> + c W<sub>y</sub>) - U<sub>y</sub> (&lambda; - 1) (a U<sub>z</sub> + b V<sub>z</sub> + c W<sub>z</sub>) : :
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;where &eta; = sqrt((&lambda; - &mu;) (&lambda; - 1)/&mu;).
</p>
