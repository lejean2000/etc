Brocard-Lemoine points: X(14941)-X(14952)
</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">

<div class="preamble-sect">
<p>
This preamble and centers X(14941)-X(14952) were contributed by CÃ©sar Eliud Lozada, October 24-27, 2017.
</p>
<p>
Let ABC be a triangle, P any point in its plane (not on its sidelines) and A', B', C' the traces of P on ABC. Let A<sub>b</sub>, A<sub>c</sub> be the points where BC is cut by the parallel to AB through B' and the parallel to AC through C', respectively. Build B<sub>c</sub>, B<sub>a</sub>, C<sub>a</sub>, C<sub>b</sub> cyclically. Then lines AA<sub>b</sub>, BB<sub>c</sub>, CC<sub>a</sub> concur at a point W<sub>1</sub> and lines AA<sub>c</sub>, BB<sub>a</sub>, CC<sub>b</sub> concur at a point W<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<p>
For P = u : v : w (trilinears), W<sub>1</sub> and W<sub>2</sub> have trilinears as shown:
<br>
&emsp;W<sub>1</sub> = c/v : a/w : b/u
<br>
&emsp;W<sub>2</sub> = b/w : c/u : a/v
</p>
<p>
Note that for P=X(6), W<sub>1</sub> and W<sub>2</sub> are the Brocard points of ABC.
</p>
<p>
The previous construction is due to Lemoine as an attempt to generalize the Brocard geometry (Roger A. Johnson, <i>Advanced Eucliden Geometry</i>, Dover, New York, 1960, &#167;499-500, pp. 299). Johnson writes, <i>these points have many properties resembling those of the Brocard points</i>.
</p>
<p>
<b>Some properties:</b><br>
<br>
&emsp;a) For P=X(2), W<sub>1</sub>=W<sub>2</sub>=X(2)<br>
<br>
&emsp;b) Points A<sub>b</sub>, A<sub>c</sub>, B<sub>c</sub>, B<sub>a</sub>, C<sub>a</sub>, C<sub>b</sub> lie on a conic (here named the <b>Brocard-Lemoine conic of P</b>) with center O(P) given by:
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;O(P) = (a^4*u^4-a*u*(b*v+c*w)*(3*b*c*v*w+a^2*u^2)-(b^2*v^2+c^2*w^2)*(b*c*v*w+3*a^2*u^2)-b*v*c*w*(-b*c*v*w+2*a^2*u^2))/a : :  (trilinears)
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;O(P) = x^4-x*(y+z)*(x^2+3*y*z)-(y^2+z^2)*(3*x^2+y*z)-y*z*(2*x^2-y*z) : : (barycentrics)<br>
<br>
&emsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; - For P=X(194), the conic is a circle centered at X(14135) and having squared-radius = &prod;(3*S^2-4*SB*SC-SW^2)/(64*R^2*S^4)<br>
<br>
&emsp;c) The trilinear pole Q(P) of the line W<sub>1</sub>W<sub>2</sub> has coordinates:
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;Q(P) = u*(b^2*v^2-a*c*u*w)*(c^2*w^2-a*b*u*v) : :  (trilinears)
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;Q(P) = x*(y^2-x*z)*(z^2-x*y) : :  (barycentrics for P=x:y:z)<br>
<br>
&emsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; - For P on the line at infinity, Q(P) = P
<br>
&emsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; - For P on the Steiner circumellipse, Q(P) = X(2)
<br>
&emsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; - For any other P, Q(Q(P)) = P, i.e., pairs {P, Q(P)} form a conjugation.<br>
<br>
&emsp;&emsp;Following the last property, Q(P) will be named here the <b>antitomic conjugate of P</b>. This conjugacy was mentioned by Randy Hutson in X(9513), although its explanation was left pending. In a personal communication (Oct 26, 2017), Hutson gives the reasons for this name:
<blockquote>
<i>
As the <b>antigonal conjugate</b> of a point P is defined as:
<br>
&emsp;1) The <u>isogonal</u> conjugate of the inverse in the circumcircle (=<u>isogonal</u> conjugate of the line-at-infinity) of the <u>isogonal</u> conjugate of P,<br>
<br>
I define the <b>antitomic conjugate</b> of P as:
<br>
&emsp;1) The <u>isotomic</u> conjugate of the inverse in the Steiner-circumellipse (=<u>isotomic</u> conjugate of the line-at-infinity) of the <u>isotomic</u> conjugate of P.
</i></blockquote>
<p>
&emsp;&emsp;Randy Hutson also notes that Q(P) is the antipode of P in the hyperbola {A,B,C,X(2),P}.
</p>
