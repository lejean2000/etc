<p>
Clifford's theorems, named after the English geometer William Kingdon Clifford, are a sequence of theorems relating to intersections of circles in general position.
</p>
<p>1st theorem: Given four circles d<sub>i</sub>, i=1..4, passing through a common point M, let P<sub>ij</sub> be the second intersection of d<sub>i</sub> and d<sub>j</sub>. Define the circles d'<sub>l</sub>={P<sub>ij</sub>, P<sub>jk</sub>, P<sub>ki</sub>} for l=1..4. Then these last four circles have a common point Q, here denoted as the <b>Clifford(4) center of circles d<sub>i</sub></b>.</p>
<p>2nd theorem: Given five circles passing through a common point M, every subset of four of these circles determines a Clifford(4) center Q<sub>i</sub> (by the first theorem). Then these five points Q<sub>i</sub> lie on a circle S whose center will be denoted as the <b>Clifford(5) center</b> of the given circles.</p>
<p>3rd theorem: Given six circles passing through a common point M, every subset of five circles determines a circle S<sub>i</sub> (by the 2nd theorem). Then these six circles have a common point, denoted here as the <b>Clifford(6) center</b> of the given circles.</p>
<p>The sequence of theorems can be continued indefinitely. (References: <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/CliffordsCircleTheorem.html">Mathworld</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford&#39;s_circle_theorems">Wikipedia</a>).</p>
