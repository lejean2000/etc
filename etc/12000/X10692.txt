Barycentrics &nbsp;&nbsp; a^2 (a^4-2 a^2 b^2+b^4-a^3 c+a^2 b c+a b^2 c-b^3 c-a^2 c^2-b^2 c^2+a c^3+b c^3) (a^4-a^3 b-a^2 b^2+a b^3+a^2 b c+b^3 c-2 a^2 c^2+a b c^2-b^2 c^2-b c^3+c^4) (a^6-3 a^4 b^2+3 a^2 b^4-b^6+2 a^4 b c+2 a^3 b^2 c-2 a^2 b^3 c-2 a b^4 c-3 a^4 c^2+2 a^3 b c^2-2 a^2 b^2 c^2+2 a b^3 c^2+b^4 c^2-2 a^2 b c^3+2 a b^2 c^3+3 a^2 c^4-2 a b c^4+b^2 c^4-c^6) :  :
<p>
Let ABC be a triangle and IaIbIc the antipedal triangle of I (i.e., the excentral triangle).  Let<br>
A' = reflections of Ia in BC, and define B' and C' cyclically<br>
Ma = midpoint of segment AA', and define Mb and Mc cyclically.
</p>
<p></p>
Then ABC and A'B'C' are cyclologic, and<br>
X(10692) = point of concurrence of circumcircles of AB'C', BC'A', CA'B'<br>
X(80) = point of concurrence of circumcircles of A'BC, B'CA, C'AB.<br>
<p></p>
Also, ABC and MaMbMc are cyclologic; let <br>
X(80) = point of concurrence of circumcircles of AMbMc, BMcMa, CMaMb<br>
X(10693) = point of concurrence of circumcircles of MaBC, MbCA, McAB.
<p></p>
See Antreas Hatzipolakis and Peter Moses, <a href="http://www.hyacinthos.epizy.com/message.php?msg=24768"> 24768</a>.
<p></p>
Let A'B'C' be the excentral triangle.  Let L, M, N be lines through A', B', C', respectively, parallel to line X(4)X(46).  Let L' be the reflection of L in BC, let M' be the reflection of M in CA, and let N' be the reflection of N in AB.  The lines L', M', N' concur in X(10692).  (Randy Hutson, December 10, 2016)
