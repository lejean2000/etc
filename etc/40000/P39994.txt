<p class="preamble-title">
Points associated with cubics: X(39994)-X(40106)
</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">
</header>
<div class="preamble-sect">
<p>
This preamble is contributed by Clark Kimberling and Peter Moses, October 26, 2020.
<p>
In the plane of a triangle ABC, suppose that P = p : q : r and U = u : v : w are distinct points such that p*q*r*u*v*w is not zero, and that the point
</p>
<p>
(b-c)*p : (c-a)*q : (a-b)*r
is on the line at infinity; i.e.,
</p>
<p>
(b-c)p(a,b,c) + (c-a)p(b,c,a) + (a-b)p(c,a,b) = 0.
</p>
<p>
Let CC(P,U) denote the cubic given by
</p>
<p>
(b + c)*p*(y*v - z*w)*(x*u + y*v)*(x*u + z*w) + (c + a)*q*(z*w - x*u)*(y*v + z*w)*(y*v + x*u) + (a + b)*r*(x*u - y*v)*(z*w + x*u)*(z*w + y*v) = 0.
</p>
<p>
It is easy to verify that the following twelve points lie on CC(P,U):
</p>
<p>
A, B, C; the vertices of the anticevian triangle of U; 1/u : 1/v : 1/w;  a/u : b/v : c/w; b*c/u : c*a/v : a*b/w, and the vertices of the cevian triangle of the point U' shown below.
</p>
<p>
Note that the first nine points are invariant of P, but that for fixed U, distinct points P yield distinct cubics CC(P,U). Thus, for fixed U, these cubics provide examples of "Cayley's Paradox" (see <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cramer%27s_theorem_(algebraic_curves)"><b>Cramer's Theorem</b></a> or
<a href="https://www.maa.org/book/export/html/117741"><b>When Nine Points Are Worth But Eight: Euler's Resolution of Cramer's Paradox.</b></a>
</p>
<p>
The cubic CC(P,U) is the cubic pK(P',U'), where
</p>
<p>
P' = v^2w^2 : w^2 u^2 : u^2 v^2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; and &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; U' = v*w*(b+c)*p - (a+c)*q - (a+b)*r : :
</p>
<p>
For a discussion of pK cubics, see <a href="https://bernard-gibert.pagesperso-orange.fr/files/isocubics.html"><b>Special Isocubics in the Triangle Plane.</b></a>.
</p>
<p>
See also the preambles just before X(39693) and X(39765).
</p>
<p>
Example 1. The cubic CC(X(10),X(86)) = Pk(X(594),X(3995)) passes through A, B, C, the vertices of the cevian triangle of X(3995), the vertices of the anticevian triangle of X(10), and X(i) for i = 10, 37, 321, 3159, 3995.  The tangents at A, B, C concur in X(40085).
</p>
<p>
Example 2. The cubic CC(X(10),X(514)) = Pk(X(1086),X(20295)) passes through A, B, C, the vertices of the cevian triangle of X(20295), the vertices of the anticevian triangle of X(514), and X(i) for i = 513, 514, 693, 14078, 20295.  The tangents at A, B, C concur in X(40086).
</p>
<p>
Example 3. The cubic CC(X(2),X(6)) = Pk(X(1502),X(75)) passes through X(i) for i = 75, 76, 561, 6382, 6383, 18832, 18837.
</p>
<p>
Example 4. The cubic CC(X(10),X(6)) = Pk(X(1502),X(40087)) passes through X(40034).
</p>
<p>
Example 5. The cubic CC(X(42),X(6)) = Pk(X(1502),X(40088)) passes through X(40005).
</p>
<p>

</p>

</p>

</p>

</p>

</p>

</p>
