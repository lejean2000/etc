<p>

In 1891, Frank Herbert Loud published the following theorem.

<div style="margin-left: 10%; margin-right: 10%;">
<p>
Let P<sub>1</sub>, P<sub>2</sub>, P<sub>3</sub>, Q<sub>1</sub>, Q<sub>2</sub>, Q<sub>3</sub>, R<sub>1</sub>, R<sub>2</sub>, R<sub>3</sub> be nine distinct points on a cubic &Psi; such that:</p>
<div style="margin-left: 3%;">
<p>
(1) P<sub>1</sub>, P<sub>2</sub>, P<sub>3</sub> are collinear on a line, L<sub>P</sub>;<br>
(2) P<sub>1</sub>, Q<sub>1</sub>, R<sub>1</sub> are collinear on a line, L<sub>1</sub>;<br>
(3) P<sub>2</sub>, Q<sub>2</sub>, R<sub>2</sub> are collinear on a line, L<sub>2</sub>;<br>
(4) P<sub>3</sub>, Q<sub>3</sub>, R<sub>3</sub> are collinear on a line, L<sub>3</sub>.<br>
</p>
</div>
<p>
Define S<sub>1</sub>, S<sub>2</sub>, S<sub>3</sub> and T<sub>1</sub>, T<sub>2</sub>, T<sub>3</sub> as follows:</p>
<div style="margin-left: 3%;">
<p>
&nbsp;&nbsp;(i)  S<sub>1</sub> = Q<sub>2</sub>R<sub>3</sub>&cap;Q<sub>1</sub>R<sub>1</sub> and T<sub>1</sub> = Q<sub>2</sub>R<sub>3</sub>&cap;&Psi; - {Q<sub>2</sub>, R<sub>3</sub>}
<br>
&nbsp;(ii)  S<sub>2</sub> = Q<sub>3</sub>R<sub>1</sub>&cap;Q<sub>2</sub>R<sub>2</sub> and T<sub>2</sub> = Q<sub>3</sub>R<sub>1</sub>&cap;&Psi; - {Q<sub>3</sub>, R<sub>1</sub>}
<br>
(iii)  S<sub>3</sub> = Q<sub>1</sub>R<sub>2</sub>&cap;Q<sub>3</sub>R<sub>3</sub> and T<sub>3</sub> = Q<sub>1</sub>R<sub>2</sub>&cap;&Psi; - {Q<sub>1</sub>, R<sub>2</sub>}
</p>
</div>
<p>Then (a) the points S<sub>1</sub>, S<sub>2</sub> and S<sub>3</sub> are collinear (on a line L<sub>S</sub> ); (b) the points T<sub>1</sub>, T<sub>2</sub> and T<sub>3</sub> are collinear (on a line L<sub>T</sub> ); and
(c) the lines L<sub>P</sub>, L<sub>S</sub> and L<sub>T</sub> concur. <br>
</p>
</div>
<p>
Reference: Loud F.H. "A theorem in plane cubics", <i>Annals of Mathematics</i>, Vol. 6, No. 1 (Jun., 1891), pp. 5-6
</p>
<br>

<p>
The point of concurrence of the lines L<sub>P</sub>, L<sub>S</sub> and L<sub>T</sub>, as described in the part (c) of the theorem, are represented here as the <b>(P<sub>1</sub>, Q<sub>1</sub>, R<sub>1</sub>)-(P<sub>2</sub>, Q<sub>2</sub>, R<sub>2</sub>)-(P<sub>3</sub>, Q<sub>3</sub>, R<sub>3</sub>)-Loud point of &Psi;</b>, using ETC indexes; e.g., X(1249) is represented by 1249.  Although the 3rd point in each triad is determined by the first 2 points, the 3rd is included for completeness.
</p>
