<p>"In every quadrilateral may be inscribed at least one square having a vertex on each of the four sides. If there is more than one such square in a given quadrilateral, there is an infinite number". Reference: Hebbert, C. M., <i>Annals of Mathematics</i>, Second Series, Vol. 16, No. 1/4 (1914 - 1915), pp. 38-42.
</p>
<p>
Let P be a point in the plane &Pi; of ABC and let &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> = A<sub>C</sub>A'<sub>B</sub>A'<sub>C</sub>A<sub>B</sub> be a square inscribed in the quadrilateral ABPC, with A<sub>C</sub> on line AB, A'<sub>B</sub> on BP, A'<sub>C</sub> on PC, and A<sub>B</sub> on CA. It is easy to prove, using homogeneous coordinates, that &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> is unique if P does not lie on the conic &kappa;<sub>A</sub> given by the trilinear equation
</p>
<p>
Sa<sup>2</sup>u<sup>2</sup> + bc(a<sup>2</sup> + S)vw + ca(b<sup>2</sup> + S)wu + ab(c<sup>2</sup> + S)uv  = 0
</p>
<p>
Note that B and C lie on &kappa;<sub>A</sub>.  Define &kappa;<sub>B</sub> and &kappa;<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  If
</p>
<p>
P &isin; &Pi; - &kappa;<sub>A</sub>&cup;&kappa;<sub>B</sub>&cup;&kappa;<sub>C</sub> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(1),
</p>
<p>
then there exist unique squares &Upsi;<sub>A</sub>, &Upsi;<sub>B</sub>, &Upsi;<sub>C</sub> inscribed in the quadrilaterals ABPC, BCPA, CAPB, respectively. If P = u : v : w (trilinears) satisfies (1), then the vertices of &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> are as shown here:
</p>
<p>
A<sub>B</sub>  = (au + bv + cw)uS + (abv + S<sub>C</sub>u)cw : 0 : bvwS + (bcv + S<sub>A</sub>w)au
<br>
A<sub>C</sub>  = (au + bv + cw)uS + (acw + S<sub>B</sub>u)bv : cvwS + (bcw + S<sub>A</sub>v)au : 0
<br>
A'<sub>B</sub> = u(auS + bS<sub>B</sub>v) : (S + S<sub>A</sub>)auv + abcuw + (S + S<sub>C</sub>)cvw : w(auS + bS<sub>B</sub>v)
<br>
A'<sub>C</sub> = u(auS + cS<sub>C</sub>w) : v(auS + cS<sub>C</sub>w) :  abcuv + (S + S<sub>A</sub>)auw + (S + S<sub>B</sub>)bvw
</p>
<p>
If P lies on the side BC of ABC then  &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> is the <b>A-inner-inscribed square</b> of ABC, as defined at <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/InnerInscribedSquaresTriangle.html">Mathworld:  Inner Inscribed Squares Triangle</a>.  The square &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> is here named here the <b>A-quadsquare triangle</b> of P. The sidelength of &Upsi;<sub>A</sub> is given by
</p>
<p>
L<sub>A</sub> = S*Sqrt(abc(2uvw(aS<sub>A</sub>u+S(bv + cw)) + bc(v^2 w^2 + u^2 w^2 + u^2 v^2)a))/|(au + cw)(au + bv)S + (avw + buw + cuv)abc|
</p>
<p>
Let O<sub>A</sub>, O<sub>B</sub>, O<sub>C</sub> be the centers of the squares &Upsi;<sub>A</sub>, &Upsi;<sub>B</sub>, &Upsi;<sub>C</sub>, respectively. The triangle O<sub>A</sub>O<sub>B</sub>O<sub>C</sub> is here named the <b>P-quadsquares triangle</b>.  The vertex O<sub>A</sub> are given by
</p>
<p>
O<sub>A</sub> = a(bcvw + 2Su^2) + (S + S<sub>B</sub>)buv + (S + S<sub>C</sub>)cuw : abcuw + (S + S<sub>A</sub>)auv + (S + S<sub>C</sub>)cvw : abcuv + (S + S<sub>A</sub>)auw + (S + S<sub>B</sub>)bvw,
</p>
<p>
and cyclically for O<sub>B</sub> and O<sub>C</sub>.
</p>
<p>
The P-quadsquares triangle is perspective to ABC for all P satisyfing (1).  The perspector is given by
</p>
<p>
Z(P) = u((S + S<sub>B</sub>)bv + (S+S<sub>C</sub>)cw) + abcvw : :
</p>
<p>
The transformation P &rarr; Z(P) maps lines onto conics. For P on the Euler line, the locus of Z(P) is the conic through X(6), X(485) and the vertices of the three inner-inscribed-squares triangle.
</p>
<p>

</p>
<p>
