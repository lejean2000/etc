<h3 id="X8043">X(8043)&nbsp;=&nbsp; FARHANGI-CYCLOCEVIAN IMAGE OF X(1)</h3>
Barycentrics &nbsp;&nbsp; a (b-c) (a^3+a^2 b-a b^2-b^3+a^2 c-a b c-3 b^2 c-a c^2-3 b c^2-c^3) : :
<p>
Suppose that P is a point in the plane of a triangle ABC.  Let DEF be the cevian triangle of P, and let O<sub>P</sub> be the circumcircle of DEF.  Let D' be the point, other than D, in AD&cap;O<sub>P</sub>, and define E' and F' cyclically.  Let P<sub>A</sub> = E'F'&cap;BC, and define P<sub>B</sub> and P<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Then
P<sub>A</sub>, P<sub>B</sub>, P<sub>C</sub> are collinear.  Let P'<sub>A</sub> = E'F'&cap;EF, and define P'<sub>B</sub> and P'<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Then P'<sub>A</sub>, P'<sub>B</sub>, P'<sub>C</sub> are collinear.  Let L(P) be the line P<sub>A</sub>P<sub>B</sub>P<sub>C</sub> and let L'(P) be the line P'<sub>A</sub>P'<sub>B</sub>P'<sub>C</sub>.  Next, suppose that Q is the cyclocevian conjugate (also called Terquem conjugate) of P.  The four lines L(P), L'(P), L(Q), L'(Q) concur in a point, introduced here as the <b>Farhangi-cyclocevian image</b> of P, denoted by f(P).  If P = X(2), then Q = X(4), and the four lines are perpendicular to the Euler line; they concur in X(523).  (Based on notes from Sohail Farhangi, August 4, 2015).
</p>
<p>
Peter Moses finds (August 5, 2015) that if P = p: q : r (barycentrics), then
</p>
<p>
f(P) = p (p q r (p ((a^2-b^2+2 c^2) q-(a^2+2 b^2-c^2) r)+ ((a^2-b^2+c^2) q^2-(a^2+b^2-c^2) r^2))-q^2 r^2((2 b^2-2 c^2) p-a^2 (q-r)) +p^2 (c^2 q^3-b^2 r^3)) : :
</p>
<p>
f(P) = p[p<sup>2</sup>q<sup>2</sup>r(a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup> + 2c<sup>2</sup>) - p<sup>2</sup>qr<sup>2</sup>(a<sup>2</sup> + 2b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>) + pq<sup>3</sup>r(a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup>) - pqr<sup>3</sup>(a<sup>2</sup> + b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>) - 2pq<sup>2</sup>r<sup>2</sup>(b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>) + q<sup>2</sup>r<sup>2</sup>(q - r)a<sup>2</sup> + p<sup>2</sup>q<sup>3</sup>c<sup>2</sup> - p<sup>2</sup>r<sup>3</sup>b<sup>2</sup>] : :
</p>

<p>
f(P) = perspector of ABC and the side-triangle of the cevian triangles of P and Q. (Randy Hutson, August 12, 2015)
</p>
<p>
If P lies on the Lucas cubic (other than X(7)), then f(P) lies on the line at infinity, and is, in fact, the infinite point of the trilinear polar of P.  (Randy Hutson, August 12, 2015)
</p>
