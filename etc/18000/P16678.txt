<p>
This section follows the discussion of collineations just before X(16286), and especially just before X(16544).  If A&prime;B&prime;C&prime; is a central triangle other than ABC and P and U are triangle centers, then (A,B,C,P; A&prime;,B&prime;,C&prime;,U) is a regular collineation, as is its inverse, given by (A&prime;,B&prime;,C&prime;,U; A,B,C,P).
</p>
<p>
The collineation images at X(16678)-X(16695) result from A&prime;B&prime;C&prime; = tangential triangle, P = X(2), and U = X(1).  We write the image of X as m(X); let m<sup>-1</sup> denote the inverse collineation.  Then centers X(16678)-X(16695) are examples of m(X), and X(16696)-X(16759) are examples of m<sup>-1</sup>(X). Other examples are given by the following list, in which the appearance of (i,j) means that m(X(i)) = X(j):
</p>
<p>
(1,8053), (2,1), (7,3185), (8,3941), (37,1621), (58,1631), (69,2352), (81,3), (85,48), (86,55), (274,6), (333,56), (348,19),  (1107,86), (2275,75),, (3121,99), (4560,513)
</p>
<p>
A collineation maps lines to lines.  The appearance of {h,i} &srarr; {j,k} in the next list means that m(X(h)X(i)) = X(j)X(k):
</p>
