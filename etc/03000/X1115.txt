<h3 id="X1115">X(1115)&nbsp;=&nbsp;EXTERIOR-ANGLE CURVATURE CENTROID <a id="Exterior-AngleCurvatureCentroid"></a></h3>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; (&pi; - A)/a : (&pi; - B)/b : (&pi; - C)/c
<br>
Barycentrics &nbsp;&nbsp;&pi; - A : &pi; - B : &pi; - C
<br>
<p class="bold">X(1115) = 9 X[2] - 5 X[18295], 3 X[360] - 5 X[18295], 6 X[18294] - 5 X[18295]</p>
<p>
X(1115) is the center of mass of a point-mass system obtained by placing
at vertex <var>A</var> a mass equal to the magnitude of the exterior
angle (that's &pi; - <var>A</var>) at <var>A</var>, and cyclically for
<var>B</var> and <var>C</var>. (<span class="author">Peter Scales</span>,
Hyacinthos #5528, 5/22/02) This description is also found in <span
class="author">Honsberger</span>, <span class="title">Episodes in
Nineteenth and Twentieth Century Eulidean Geometry</span>, p. 120, where
it is mistakenly attributed to the Steiner point, X(99).
</p>
<p>
X(1115) should not be confused with Jakob Steiner's actual "Curvature Centroid"
(discovered in 1825), applicable to all polygons, obtained as the weighted
sum of vertices with weights determined by sines of doubled vertex angles
[1]. Remarkably, the pedal polygon of a polygon with respect to this point
has extremal area: it is minimal (resp. maximal) if the sign of the sum of
sines of double angles is negative (resp. positive) <span class="label">[1]</span>.  For a triangle,
this point is the circumcenter X(3) and the extremal pedal triangle is the
medial triangle. (Dan Reznik, July 7, 2020).</p>
<p>
<span class="label">[1]</span> <span class="author">Jakob Steiner</span>,
<span class="title">"Krümmungs Schwerpunkt ebener Curven"</span>,
<span class="journal">Abhandlungen der Königlich Preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften</span>,
1838. (Dan Reznick, July 7, 2020)
</p>
