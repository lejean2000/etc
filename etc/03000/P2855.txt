SM Points

<div class="preamble-sect">
Centers X(2855) to X(2868) are examples of Simson-Moses points, defined
by changing "isogonal" to "isotomic" in the definition of Simson-Ribgy
points (X(2687) to X(2770). That is, for points P and U on the
circumcircle of triangle ABC, the point of intersection of the lines (P
and isotomic conjugate of U) and (U and isotomic conjugate of P)
defines the Simson-Moses point of P and U, denoted by SM(P,U). If P and
U are given in barycentric coordinates p : q : r and u : v : w, then
</p>
(rv - qw)/(qv - rw) : (pw - ru)/(rw - pu) : (qu - pv)/(pu - qv)
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(barycentrics)
</p>
<br>
If P and U are given in trilinear coordinates p : q : r and u : v : w,
then
</p>
b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup>(rv - qw)/(b<sup>2</sup>qv -
c<sup>2</sup>rw) : c<sup>2</sup>a<sup>2</sup>(pw - ru)/(c<sup>2</sup>rw
- a<sup>2</sup>pu) : a<sup>2</sup>b<sup>2</sup>(qu -
pv)/(a<sup>2</sup>pu - b<sup>2</sup>qv)
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(trilinears)
</p>
<br>
On Oct. 23, 2004, Peter Moses described the point for which the
designation SM(P,U) is here introduced, and he noted that SM(P,U), like
SR(P,U), lies on the circumcircle.

RS(X(i),X(j)) = X(K):</b></p>

<p>The definitions of SR and SM points depend on two kinds of
isoconjugates (i.e., isogonal and isotomic). To generalize, suppose</p>

</p>
X = x : y : z, P = p : q : r, U = u : v : w
</p>
<br>
are points, none on a sideline of triangle ABC. Then
</p>
(X-isoconjugate of P) = yzqr : zxrp :
xypq&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; and&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
(X-isoconjugate of U) = yzvw : zxwu : xyuv,
</p>
<br>
Denote these points by P(X) and U(X), respectively. It is easy to show
using determinants that PU(X) and UP(X) meet in the point. That point
defines the <i>X-Simson point of P and U</i>, denoted by S(X;P,U):
</p>
S(X;P,U) = yz(qw - rv)/(qvy - rwz) : zx(ru - pw)/(rwz - pux) :
xy(pv - qu)/(pux - qvy).
</p>
<br>
<b>Theorem: If P and U are on the circumcircle, then S(X;P,U) is on the
circumcircle.</b> A "computer proof" is easy: solve the equations a/p +
b/q + c/r = 0 and a/u + b/v + c/w = 0 for r and w, substitute for these
in a/r + b/s + c/t, where r, s, t are trilinears for S(X;P,U), and find
that a/r + b/s + c/t = 0.
<p>To conclude, SR(P,U) = S(X(1);P,U) and SM(P,U) = S(X(2);P,U).</p>
</p>
