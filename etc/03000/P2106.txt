<p>Triangle centers on a cubic yield non-central points on the cubic; e.g., if P and P' are on EAC2, then the line of PP' meets EAC2 in third
point, L(P,P'), on EAC2. (Possibly L(P,P') is P or P'.) If A', B', C'
are central triangle vertices and X is a triangle center, and all four points A', B', C', and X lie on EAC2, then the
points</p>

<p>
L(A',X),&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; L(B',X),&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; L(C',X)

<p>are central triangle vertices on EAC2. (A definition of central
triangle is given in the Glossary.) In the following table, column 1
indicates the A-vertex of such a triangle; column 2 two points
collinear with that vertex; and column 3, the A-vertex of the
X(2)-isoconjugate of the triangle. Taking row 1 as an example, <b>- a :
c :</b> represents the point <b>-a : c : b</b>, which is the A-vertex
of the central triangle with B-vertex <b>c : - b : a</b> and C-vertex
<b>b : a : - c</b>. The A-vertex is collinear with A and X(2) (so that
the B-vertex is collinear with B and X(2), etc.). Then, in column 3,
<b>- bc : a<sup>2</sup>b :</b> represents the A-vertex of the
X(2)-isoconjugate of <b>- a : c :</b> . The vertices <b>- a : c :</b>
and <b>- bc : a<sup>2</sup>b :</b> are collinear with the pivot,
X(238).</p>
