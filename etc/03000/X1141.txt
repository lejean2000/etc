<h3 id="X1141">X(1141)&nbsp;=&nbsp;GIBERT POINT</h3>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; bc/[16D<sup>2</sup> + (a<sup>2</sup>+c<sup>2</sup>-b<sup>2</sup>)(a<sup>2</sup>+b<sup>2</sup>-c<sup>2</sup>)][16D<sup>2</sup> - 3(b<sup>2</sup>+c<sup>2</sup>-a<sup>2</sup>)<sup>2</sup>] : : , where D = area(ABC) = sec(B - C)/(1 - 4 cos<sup>2</sup>A) : sec(C - A)/(1 - 4
cos<sup>2</sup>B) : sec(A - B)/(1 - 4 cos<sup>2</sup>C) (Eric Weisstein, Nov. 17, 2005)
<b><br>
X(1141) = 3X(549) - 2X(6592)
</b>
<p>
X(1141) was first noted (Hyacinthos #1498, September 25, 2000) by Bernard Gibert as a point of intersection of the circumcircle and certain cubic, denoted K<sub>n</sub>. To define K<sub>n</sub>, note first that the Neuberg cubic is the locus of a point M such that the reflections of M in the sidelines of triangle ABC are the vertices of a triangle perspective to ABC. The locus of the perspector is the cubic K<sub>n</sub>, and X(1141) is the point, other than A,B,C, in which K<sub>n</sub> meets the circumcircle. Also, X(1141) is the perspector when M = X(1157).
</p>
<p>
In <b>Jean-Pierre Ehrmann and Bernard Gibert,</b> "Special Isocubics," downloadable from <a href="http://perso.wanadoo.fr/bernard.gibert/index.html" style="font-weight: bold">Cubics in the Triangle Plane</a>, the point X(1141) is labeled E, barycentrics are given, and it is established that this point also lies on the line X(5)-to-X(110) [listed below as 5,49], two other cubics, and the hyperbola that passes through the points A, B, C, X(4), X(5).
</p>
<p>
Let A' be the reflection of A in line BC, and define B' and C' cyclically. Let A<sub>B</sub> be the reflection of A' in AB, and define A<sub>C</sub>, B<sub>C</sub>, B<sub>A</sub>, C<sub>A</sub>, C<sub>B</sub> cyclically. Let
</p>

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;A<sub>1</sub> =

</p>
<p>
Then triangle A<sub>n</sub>B<sub>n</sub>C<sub>n</sub> is perspective
to ABC, for n = 1,2,3,4,5,6. The six perspectors are X(1141), X(186), X(4), X(54), X(265), X(5), respectively. (Keith Dean, #4953, 3/12/02; coordinates by Paul Yiu, #4963; summary by Dean, #4971)
</p>
<p>
X(1141) lies on the conic of {A, B, C, X(3), X(49)}, the conic of
{A, B, C, X(6), X(567)}, and the conic of {A, B, C, X(70), X(253),
X(254)}.
</p>
<p>
X(1141) is the antipode of X(930) on the circumcircle, and X(1141) lies on the line of the nine-point center, X(5), and its isogonal conjugate, X(54).
</p>
