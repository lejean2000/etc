<p class="preamble-title">
RS Points and SR Points
</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">
</header>
<div class="preamble-sect">
<p>
Centers X(2677) to X(2770) are examples of Rigby-Simson points and
Simson-Rigby points. These two types of points are discussed in
</p>
<p><span class="author">Ross Honsberger</span>,
<span class="title">Episodes in Nineteenth and Twentieth Century Euclidean Geometry</span>,
Mathematical Association of America,
1995. Chapter 11: The Orthopole, pages 124-136.</p>
<p>Specifically, on pages 132-136, Honsberger defines the "Rigby
Point," in honor of John Rigby, as the orthopole of the six sides of
two triangles, all of whose vertices lie on a circle, and he notes that
this orthopole lies on the six Simson lines of the vertices.</p>
<p>Now suppose that P = p : q : r (trilinears) and U = u : v : w are
distinct points on the circumcircle of a triangle ABC. Their Simson
lines meet in a point X which is here called the Rigby-Simson point of
P and U, denoted by RS(P,U). That is, RS(P,U) is the orthopole of line
PU with respect to triangle ABC.</p>

<p>On the circumcircle, there is a unique point K whose Simson line is
perpendicular to line PU, and whose Simson line also passes through
RS(P,U). The point K is here called the Simson-Rigby point of P and U,
denoted by SR(P,U).  Note that SR(P,U) = &Lambda;(P,U)
</p>
<p>
If you have The Geometer's Sketchpad, you can view <a href="RigbySimson.gsp" style="font-weight: bold">RS(P,U) and SR(P,U)</a>.
</p>
<p>
<p>Continuing with contributions of Peter Moses during October, 2004, trilinears for RS(P,U) are</p>
</p>
f(a,b,c,p,q,r,u,v,w) : f(b,c,a,q,r,p,v,w,u) :  f(c,a,b,r,p,q,w,u,v), where
<p>
f(a,b,c,p,q,r,u,v,w) = 1/[a<sup>2</sup>r<sup>2</sup>v<sup>2</sup>(q cos A - p cos B)(w cos A - u cos C)] -
1/[a<sup>2</sup>q<sup>2</sup>w<sup>2</sup>(r cos A - p cos C)(v cos A -  u cos B)],</p>
</p>
<br>
and trilinears for SR(P,U), reckoned as the point of intersection of
lines P-to-U <sup>- 1</sup> and U-to-P <sup>- 1</sup>, are
</p>
(rv - qw)/(qv - rw) : (pw - ru)/(rw - pu) : (qu - pv)/(pu - qv).
</p>
