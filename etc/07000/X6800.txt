<p>
Let P be a point in the plane of a triangle ABC.  Let P<sub>A</sub> be the reflection of P in BC, and define P<sub>B</sub> and P<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Let P<sub>AB</sub> be the reflection of P in BP<sub>A</sub>, and define P<sub>BC</sub> and P<sub>CA</sub> cyclically.  Let P<sub>AC</sub> be the reflection of P in CP<sub>A</sub>, and define P<sub>BA</sub> and P<sub>CB</sub> cyclically.
At
<a href="http://www.hyacinthos.epizy.com/message.php?msg=23148" class="bold">Hyacinthos 23148</a> (March 6, 2015) Antreas Hatzipolakis asks for the locus of P such that the perpendicular bisectors of the segements P<sub>AB</sub>P<sub>AC</sub>, P<sub>BC</sub>P<sub>BA</sub>, P<sub>CA</sub>P<sub>CB</sub> concur and for the locus of the point Z(P) of concurrence.  C&eacute;sar Lozada responds that the first locus is the the union of {A,B,C} and the Euler line of ABC, and that the second locus is the line of the points X(3), X(74), and X(110).  The appearance of (i,j) in the following list means that Z(X(i)) = X(j):  (2,6800), (3,3), (4,3), (5,5944), (25,6090).  The point Z(P), for P = t*|OH| and X = X(110), is given by Z(P) = t(t - 1)|OQ|/[t - (r/|OH|)]<sup>2</sup>.
</p>
