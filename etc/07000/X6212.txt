<p>
It is a well known property of a cyclic quadrilateral that the incenters and excenters of the component triangles form a rectangular grid.  Let P<sub>A</sub> be the unique point on circumcircle arc BC such that the rectangle formed by the incenter of P<sub>A</sub>BC and the A-excenters of ABC, P<sub>A</sub>CA, and P<sub>A</sub>AB is a square; define P<sub>B</sub> and P<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Let J<sub>A</sub>, J<sub>B</sub>, J<sub>C</sub> be the excenters of ABC.  Let I<sub>A</sub> be the incenter of P<sub>A</sub>BC, I<sub>B</sub> the incenter of AP<sub>B</sub>C, Ic the incenter of ABP<sub>C</sub>.
</p>
<p>
 Let (AB) = A-excenter of P<sub>A</sub>BC and (AC) = A-excenter of P<sub>A</sub>AB.  Define (BC) and (CA) cyclically, and define (BA) and (CB) cyclically.
</p>
<p>
Let L<sub>A</sub> be the extended diagonal (AB)(AC) of square I<sub>A</sub>(AB)J<sub>A</sub>(AC), and define L<sub>B</sub> and L<sub>C</sub> cyclically.
</p>
<p>
Let A&prime; = L<sub>B</sub>&cap;L<sub>C</sub>, B&prime; = L<sub>C</sub>&cap;L<sub>A</sub>, C&prime; = L<sub>A</sub>&cap;L<sub>B</sub>.  The lines AA&prime;, BB&prime;, CC&prime; concur in X(6212).  Also, X(6212) is the orthocenter of A&prime;B&prime;C&prime;.  See also X(6213).  The trilinear polar of X(6212) passes through X(1459). (Randy Hutson, January 29, 2015)
</p>
