<p>
It is a well known property of a cyclic quadrilateral that the incenters of the component triangles form a rectangle.  Let Q<sub>A</sub> be the unique point on circumcircle arc BC such that the rectangle formed by the incenters of ABC, Q<sub>A</sub>BC, Q<sub>A</sub>CA, Q<sub>A</sub>AB  is a square.  Define Q<sub>B</sub> and Q<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Let I = X(1), and let </p>
<p>
[AA] = incenter of Q<sub>A</sub>BC, [AB] = incenter of Q<sub>A</sub>CA, [AC] = incenter of Q<sub>A</sub>AB,
<br>
[BA] = incenter of Q<sub>B</sub>CA, [BB] = incenter of Q<sub>B</sub>AB, [BC] = incenter of Q<sub>B</sub>BC,
<br>
[CA] = incenter of Q<sub>C</sub>AB, [CB] = incenter of Q<sub>C</sub>BC, [CC] = incenter of Q<sub>C</sub>CA.
</p>
<p>
Let M<sub>A</sub> be the extended diagonal [AB][AC] of square I[AA][AB][AC]], and define M<sub>B</sub> and M<sub>C</sub> cyclically.  Let A&Prime; = M<sub>B</sub>&cap;M<sub>C</sub>, B&Prime; = M<sub>C</sub>&cap;M<sub>A</sub>, C&Prime; = M<sub>A</sub>&cap;M<sub>B</sub>.  The lines AA&Prime;, BB&Prime;, CC&Prime; concur in X(6213), and the trilinear polar of X(6213) passes through X(1459).  See also X(6212).  (Randy Hutson, January 29, 2015)
</p>
<p>
