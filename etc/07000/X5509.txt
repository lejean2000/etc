<h3 id="X5509">X(5509)&nbsp;=&nbsp; KIRIKAMI SIX-CIRCLES-IMAGE OF X(31)</h3>
Barycentrics&nbsp;&nbsp; f(a,b,c) : f(b,c,a) : f(c,a,b),
where f(a,b,c) = a(b - c)<sup>2</sup>(a<sup>3</sup> - b<sup>2</sup>c -
bc<sup>2</sup>)(a<sup>2</sup>(b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> + bc) -
b<sup>4</sup> - c<sup>4</sup> - b<sup>3</sup>c - bc<sup>3</sup>)
&nbsp;&nbsp; (Seichii Kirikami, July 2, 2013)
<p>
The Kirikami six circles image of P is simply the center of the rectangular hyperbola {{A, B, C, X(4), P}}.
</p>
<p>
Let P be a point in the plane of triangle ABC. Let H<sub>A</sub> be
the orthocenter of triangle PBC, and define H<sub>B</sub> and
H<sub>C</sub> cyclically. The nine-point circles of the six triangles
H<sub>A</sub>BC, H<sub>B</sub>CA, H<sub>C</sub>AB,
AH<sub>B</sub>H<sub>C</sub>, BH<sub>C</sub>H<sub>A</sub>,
CH<sub>A</sub>H<sub>B</sub> concur in a point, Q = Q(P), the Kirikami
six-circles-image of P. Let P be given by barycentrics p : q : r. Then
Q(P) is given by
</p>
<p>
Q = h(a,b,c,p,q,r) : h(b,c,a,q,r,p) : h(c,a,b,r,p,q), where
h(a,b,c,p,q,r) = p[(a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup>)q -
(a<sup>2</sup> + b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>)r][(pr +
qr)b<sup>2</sup> - (pq + rq)c<sup>2</sup>].
</p>
<p>
The point Q lies on the nine-point circle of ABC. If P = X(31), then
Q = X(5509). If P = X(1), then Q = X(11); if P = X(2), then Q = X(115);
if P = X(3), then Q = X(125). &nbsp;&nbsp; (Seichii Kirikami, July 2,
2013)
</p>
<p>
Q maps each right circumhyperbola onto its center. Special cases: Q
maps the Feuerbach hyperbola onto X(11), the Kiepert hyperbola onto
X(115), and the Jerabek hyperbola onto X(125). &nbsp;&nbsp; (Peter
Moses, July 7, 2013)
</p>
<p>
The Kirikami six-circles-image, Q(P), of a point P is also the point
of concurrence of the nine-point circles of BCP, CAP, ABP (these being
the same as the nine-point circles of BCH<sub>A</sub>, CAH<sub>B</sub>,
ABH<sub>C</sub>). Also, Q(P) is the center of the rectangular circumhyperbola
passing through P, and Q(P) lies on the cevian circle of P.
&nbsp;&nbsp; (Randy Hutson, July 15, 2013)
</p>
<p>
The Kirikami six-circles-image of P is also the QA-P2 center
(Euler-Poncelet Point) of the quadrangle ABCP; see
<a href="http://www.chrisvantienhoven.nl/quadrangle-objects/10-mathematics/quadrangle-objects/12-qa-p2.html" style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold">Encyclopedia of Quadri-Figures</a>.
</p>
<p>
If you have The Geometer's Sketchpad, you can view
<a href="X(5509).gsp" class="bold">X(5509)</a>.
</p>
<p>
The Kirikami six circles configuration led to a conjecture by
Antreas Hatzipolakis (July 5, 2013), proved by Peter Moses, and stated
here as the Hatzipolakis-Moses Theorem: <i>Suppose that P and P* are an
isogonal conjugate pair of points in the plane of triangle ABC. Let
H<sub>A</sub> be the orthocenter of triangle PBC, and define
H<sub>B</sub> and H<sub>C</sub> cyclically. Let H'<sub>A</sub> be the
orthocenter of triangle P*BC, and define H'<sub>B</sub> and
H'<sub>C</sub> cyclically. Then circumcircles of
H<sub>A</sub>H<sub>B</sub>H<sub>C</sub> concur and the circumcircles of
H'<sub>A</sub>H'<sub>B</sub>H'<sub>C</sub> concur.</i>
</p>
<p>
The known proof of the theorem depends on a Mathematica program that
runs for several minutes. Barycentrics for most choices of P are too
long to be included here. An exception is P = X(3), for which P* = X(4)
and the two points of concurrence are H(3) = X(265) and H(4) =
X(5504).
</p>
<p>
See
<a href=" http://www.hyacinthos.epizy.com/message.php?msg=21992">Hyacinthos #21992</a>.
</p>
<p>
Let P&prime; be the isogonal conjugate of P.  Then the Kirikami-six-circles image of P is the orthopole of line X(3)P&prime;, which is also the crosssum of the circumcircle intercepts of line X(3)P&prime;.  (Randy Hutson, March 29, 2020)
</p>
<p>
X(5509) lies on these lines: {2,185}, {31,5508}, {115,3271}
</p>
<p>

X(5509) = orthopole of line X(3)X(75)<br>
X(5509) = center of hyperbola {{A,B,C,X(4),X(31)}}<br>
</p>
