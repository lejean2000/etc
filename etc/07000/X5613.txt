<p>
Suppose that P is a point. The P-Fuhrmann triangle is the triangle
A&Prime;B&Prime;C&Prime;, where A&Prime; is the reflection in line BC of the A-vertex of
the cercumcevian triangle of P, and B&Prime; and C&Prime; are defined cyclically
(so that taking P to be X(1), X(4), and X(6) yields the classical
Fuhrmann triangle, the single-point triangle X(4), and the 4th Brocard
triangle, respectively. The X(16)-Fuhrmann triangle is equilateral, and
X(5613) is its center. X(5613) is also the {X(2),X(1352)}-harmonic
conjugate of X(5617). (Randy Hutson, July 7, 2014)
</p>
<p>
Let BA&prime;C be the equilateral triangle with side BC and A&prime; on the side of BC that includes A.  Let La be the line through A&prime; parallel to BC, and define Lb and Lc cyclically.  Let A&Prime; = Lb&cap;Lc, and define B&Prime;and C&Prime; cyclically.  The Euler lines of B&prime;A&Prime;C&prime;, C&prime;B&Prime;A&prime;, A&prime;C&Prime;B&prime; concur in X(5613).  The acute angle between each pair of these Euler lines is &pi;/6.  See Dao Thanh Oai,
<a href="http://www.adgeom.epizy.com/message.php?msg=4030">
<b>ADGEOM 4030</b></a>), and Francisco Javier García Capitán,
<a href="http://www.adgeom.epizy.com/message.php?msg=4031">
<b>ADGEOM 4031</b></a>).  See also X(5617) and X(14144).
</p>
