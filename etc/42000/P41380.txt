Centers of antiparallels conics: X(41380)-X(41388)

<p>
This preamble and centers X(41380)-X(41388) were contributed by C&eacute;sar Eliud Lozada, February 22, 2021.
</p>
<p><i>Draw antiparallels through the symmedian point K. The points where these lines intersect the sides then lie on a circle, known as the cosine circle (or sometimes the second Lemoine circle)</i>. (Reference: <a href="https://mathworld.wolfram.com/CosineCircle.html">Weisstein, Eric W. "Cosine Circle." From MathWorld--A Wolfram Web Resource</a>)
<p>More generally, if P is a point on the Jerabek circumhyperbola of a triangle ABC then the antiparallel lines through P cut the sidelines of the triangle in six points lying on a conic, here named the <b>antiparallels conic of P</b> and denoted as &#120129;(P).</p>
<p>The appearance of (i, j) in the following list means that the center of &#120129;(X(i)) is X(j):<br>
&emsp;(3, 37864), (4, 6), (6, 6), (54, 41380), (64, 33584), (65, 41381), (66, 41382), (67, 41383), (68, 41384), (69, 41385), (70, 41386), (71, 41387), (72, 41388), (74, 40355).<p>
<p>&#120129;(P) is  a circle for P=X(6) and a degenerated conic for P in {X(1987), X(8678), X(8679)}. &#120129;(P) is never a parabola.</p>
