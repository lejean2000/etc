<h3 id="X356">X(356)&nbsp;=&nbsp;MORLEY CENTER</h3>
<a id="MorleyCenter"></a>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; cos A/3 + 2 cos B/3 cos C/3 : :
<br>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; = cos(B/3 - C/3) + sqrt(3)sin(A/3 + &pi;/3) (M. Stevanovic, 12/25/2007)
<br>
<b>
X(356) = X(3276) + X(3277) - X(3)
</b>
<p>
<p>X(356) is the centroid of the Morley equilateral triangle. For a discussion of the theorem and extensive list of references, see
</p>
<p>
<b>C. O. Oakley and J. C. Baker,</b> "The Morley trisector theorem," <i>American Mathematical Monthly</i> 85 (1978) 737-745.
</p>
<p>For a sketch of the Morley cubic and list of centers on it, including X(356), X(357), X(358), see
<a href="http://perso.wanadoo.fr/bernard.gibert/Exemples/morley.html">Bernard Gibert's site</a>.
</p>
<p>
See also Dao Thanh Oai, <a href="http://geometry-math-journal.ro/pdf/Volume7-Issue2/5.pdf" class="bold">Some new equilateral triangles in a plane geometry.</a>
</p>
<p>
Let A'B'C' be the 1st Morley triangle.  Let A&Prime; be the cevapoint of B' and C', and define B&Prime; and C&Prime; cyclically.  The lines AA&Prime;, BB&Prime;, CC&Prime; concur in X(356).  (Randy Hutson, September 14, 2016)
</p>
<p>
If you have The Geometer's Sketchpad, you can view <a href="X(356).gsp" class="bold">X(356)</a>.
</p>
<p>
For a biographical sketch, including details about Morley's famous theorem on angle trisectors, with history and references, see
</p>
<p>
<a href="http://faculty.evansville.edu/ck6/bstud/morley.html">Frank Morley (1860-1937) geometer</a>.
</p>
<p>

</p>
