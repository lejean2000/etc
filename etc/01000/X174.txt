<h3 id="X174">X(174)&nbsp;=&nbsp;YFF CENTER OF CONGRUENCE<a id="YffCenterOfCongruence"></a></h3>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; sec A/2 : sec B/2 : sec C/2
<br>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; [bc/(b + c - a)]<sup>1/2</sup> : [ca(c + a - b)]<sup>1/2</sup> : [ab(a + b - c)]<sup>1/2</sup>
<br>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; csc A' : csc B' : csc C', where A'B'C' = excentral triangle
<br>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; |AA'| : |BB'| : |CC'|, where A'B'C' = excentral triangle
<br>
Trilinears &nbsp;&nbsp; (csc A')(cos B' + cos C') : :, where A'B'C' = excentral triangle
<br>
Barycentrics&nbsp;&nbsp;sin A/2 : sin B/2 : sin C/2
<p>
Let Ea be the ellipse with B and C as foci and passing through X(1), and define Eb and Ec cyclically.  Let La be the line tangent to Ea at X(1), and define Lb and Lc cyclically.  Let A' = La&cap;BC, B' = Lb&cap;CA, C' = Lc&cap;AB.  Then A', B', C' are collinear, and X(174) = trilinear pole of line A'B'C'.  The line A'B'C' meets the line at infinity at the isogonal conjugate of X(3659).   Alternately, let A&Prime; be the trilinear pole of line La, and define B&Prime; and C&Prime; cyclically.  The lines AA&Prime;, BB&Prime; and CC&Prime; concur at X(174); see also X(188).  The points A&Prime;, B&Prime;, C&Prime; lie on the circumconic centered at X(9).  (Randy Hutson, December 10, 2016)
</p>
<p>Let A'B'C' be the excentral triangle.  X(174) is the trilinear pole of the Monge line of the incircles of BCA', CAB', ABC'.  (Randy Hutson, December 10, 2016)
</p>
<p>In notes dated 1987, Yff raises this question concerning certain triangles lying within ABC: can isoscelizers (defined at X(173)), P<sub>B</sub>Q<sub>C</sub>, P<sub>C</sub>Q<sub>A</sub>, P<sub>A</sub>Q<sub>B</sub>, be constructed so that, on putting
</p>

<p>
R<sub>A</sub> = P<sub>A</sub>Q<sub>B</sub>&cap;P<sub>B</sub>Q<sub>C</sub>, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; R<sub>B</sub> = P<sub>B</sub>Q<sub>C</sub>&cap;P<sub>C</sub>Q<sub>A</sub>,&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; R<sub>C</sub> = P<sub>C</sub>Q<sub>A</sub>&cap;P<sub>A</sub>Q<sub>B</sub>,
</p>
<p>
the following four triangles are congruent:
</p>
<p>
P<sub>A</sub>Q<sub>A</sub>R<sub>A</sub>, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; P<sub>B</sub>Q<sub>B</sub>R<sub>B</sub>, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
P<sub>C</sub>Q<sub>C</sub>R<sub>C</sub>, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; R<sub>A</sub>R<sub>B</sub>R<sub>C</sub> ?
</p>
<p>
After proving that the answer is yes, Yff moves the three isoscelizers in such a way that the three outer triangles, stay congruent and the inner triangle (called the Yff central triangle), R<sub>A</sub>R<sub>B</sub>R<sub>C</sub>, shrinks to X(174).
</p>
<p>
Let D be the point on side BC such that (angle BID) = (angle DIC), and likewise for point E on side CA and point F on side AB. The lines AD, BE, CF concur in X(174).  (Seiichi Kirikami, Jan. 29, 2010)
</p>
<p>
Generalization: if I is replaced by an arbitrary point P = p : q : r (trilinears), then the lines AD, BE, CF concur in the point K(P) = f(p,q,r,A) : f(q,r,p,B) : f(r,p,q,C), where f(p,q,r,A) = (q<sup>2</sup> + r<sup>2</sup> + 2qr cos A)<sup>-1/2</sup>. Moreover, if P* is the inverse of P in the circumcircle, then K(P*) = K(P). (Peter Moses, Feb. 1, 2010, based on Seiichi Kirikami's construction of X(174))
</p>
<p>
X(174) is the homothetic center of ABC and the extangents triangle of the intouch triangle. (Randy Hutson, 9/23/2011)
</p>
