Trilinears&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; f(a,b,c)
: f(b,c,a) : f(c,a,b), where f(a,b,c) = a(b + c)[a<sup>4</sup>(b + c) -
b<sup>4</sup>(c + a) - c<sup>4</sup>(a + b) +
b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup>(b + c) + c<sup>2</sup>a<sup>2</sup>(a - c) +
a<sup>2</sup>b<sup>2</sup>(a - b) - 2ab<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup>]<br>
Barycentrics&nbsp;&nbsp; af(a,b,c) : bf(b,c,a) : cf(c,a,b)

<p>In 2008, Alexei Myakishev gave the following construction of a
conic. Let C<sub>A</sub> and C<sub>B</sub> be points on the line AB
satisfying |BC<sub>A</sub>| = |CB| and |AC<sub>B</sub>| = |CA| and
arranged in this order: C<sub>A</sub>, B, A, and C<sub>B</sub>. Define
points A<sub>B</sub>, C<sub>B</sub>, B<sub>C</sub>, A<sub>C</sub>
cyclically. The six points C<sub>A</sub>, B<sub>A</sub>, A<sub>B</sub>,
C<sub>B</sub>, B<sub>C</sub>, A<sub>C</sub> lie on a conic. Myakishev's
proof is by Carnot's theorem, since</p>

<p>[c/(c+a)][(a+b)/b][a/(a+b)][(b+c)/c][b/(b+c)][(c+a)/a] = 1.</p>

<p>For details, in Russian, visit Item 4 at <a href=
"http://www.geometry.ru/persons/myakishev/text.htm" style=
"font-style: italic">Geometry.ru</a>,</p>

lines constructed from points numbered 1,3,4,6,8,9,10,11, as
follows:</p>

<p>X(3588) = X(8)X(573)&cap;X(2269,3057), where</p>

</p>
