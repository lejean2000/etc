<h3 id="X3078">X(3078)&nbsp;=&nbsp;DANNEELS POINT OF X(5)</h3>
Trilinears&nbsp;&nbsp; a[cos<sup>2</sup>(B - C)][b cos(C - A) + c cos (A - B)] : :
<br>
Barycentrics&nbsp;&nbsp; a<sup>2</sup>[cos<sup>2</sup>(B - C)][b cos(C - A) + c cos (A - B)] : :
<p>
Let A'B'C' be the cevian triangle of a point X = x : y : z (trilinears), let L(A) be the line through A parallel to B'C', and define L(B) and L(C) cyclically. The lines L(A), L(B), L(C) form a triangle homothetic to A'B'C', with homothetic center
</p>
<p>ax<sup>2</sup>(by + cz) : by<sup>2</sup>(cz + ax) : cz<sup>2</sup>(ax + by).
</p>
<p>We denote this point by D(X) and call it the Danneels point of X. (Eric Danneels, Hyacinthos, Jan. 28, 2005).  In addition to the discussion below, see the preamble to X(8012) and the Danneels points beginning at X(8012).
</p>
<p>
If X lies on the line at infinity, then D(X) = X(2) of cevian triangle of X.  (Randy Hutson, Jul 23, 2015)
</p>
<p>
The formula is simpler in barycentrics: if U = u : v : w, then
</p>
<p>
D(U) = u<sup>2</sup>(v + w) : v<sup>2</sup>(w + u) : w<sup>2</sup>(u + v).
<br>
<p>If X is line the Euler line, then D(X) is on
the Euler line. A proof follows. Let a<sub>1</sub>, b<sub>1</sub>,
c<sub>1</sub> be cos A, cos B, cos C, respectively, so that the Euler
line is given parametrically as x(t) : y(t) : z (t) by
</p>
<p>
&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; x = b<sub>1</sub>c<sub>1</sub> +
ta<sub>1</sub><br>
&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; y = c<sub>1</sub>a<sub>1</sub> +
tb<sub>1</sub><br>
&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; z = a<sub>1</sub>b<sub>1</sub> +
tc<sub>1</sub>,</p>

<p>where t is an arbitrary function homogenous of degree 0 in a, b, c.
Then D(X) is the point U = u : v : w given by</p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; u = ax<sup>2</sup>(by + cz)<br>
&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; v = by<sup>2</sup>(cz + ax)<br>
&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; w = cz<sup>2</sup>(ax + by).</p>

<p>A point P = p : q : r is on the Euler line if</p>

<p>a(b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>)(b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> -
a<sup>2</sup>)p<br>
+ b(c<sup>2</sup> - a<sup>2</sup>)(c<sup>2</sup> + a<sup>2</sup> -
b<sup>2</sup>)q<br>
+ c(a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup>)(a<sup>2</sup> + b<sup>2</sup>
-c<sup>2</sup>)r = 0.<br>
</p>

<p>It is easy to check that the point U satisfies this equation. (This
sort of algebraic proof is more inclusive than a geometric proof,
because here, a,b,c are indeterminates or variables. They can, for
example, take values that are not sidelengths of a triangle. Here, cos
A is defined as (b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> - a<sup>2</sup>)/(2bc),
so that no dependence on a geometric angle is necessary.)
</p>
