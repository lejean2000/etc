<p class="preamble-title">
Central Triangles and More Combos
</p>
<img src="rightri.svg" style="height:30px" alt="rightri">
</header>
<div class="preamble-sect">

<p>Many triangle centers can be defined (as just
above) by the form Xcom(nT), where T denotes a central triangle. In
order to present such centers, it is helpful to introduce the notation
T(f(a,b,c), g(b,c,a)) for central triangles. Following TCCT, pages
53-54, suppose that each of f(a,b,c) and g(a,b,c) is a center-function
or the zero function, and that one of these three conditions holds:</p>

<p>the degree of homogeneity of g equals that of f;<br>
f is the zero function and g is not the zero function;<br>
g is the zero function and f is not the zero function.</p>

<p>There are two cases to be considered: If g(a,b,c)=g(a,c,b), then the
central triangle T(f(a,b,c), g(b,c,a)) is defined by the following 3x3
matrix (whose rows give homogeneous coordinates for the A-, B-, C-
vertices, respectively):</p>

<p>f(a,b,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; g(b,c,a)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
g(c,a,b)<br>
g(a,b,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; f(b,c,a)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
g(c,a,b)<br>
g(a,b,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; g(b,c,a)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
f(c,a,b)</p>

<p>If g(a,b,c) is not equal to g(a,c,b), then the central triangle
T(f(a,b,c),g(b,c,a)) is defined by the following 3x3 matrix:</p>

<p>f(a,b,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; g(b,c,a)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
g(c,b,a)<br>
g(a,c,b)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; f(b,c,a)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
g(c,a,b)<br>
g(a,b,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; g(b,a,c)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
f(c,a,b)</p>

<p>If the homogenous coordinates are chosen to be barycentric, as they
are for present purposes, then the new notation for central triangles
is typified by these examples:</p>

<p><b>Medial triangle</b> = T(1, 0)<br>
<b>Anticomplementary triangle</B> = T(-1, 1)<br>
<b>Incentral triangle</b> = T(a, 0)<br>
<b>Excentral triangle</b> = T(-a, b)<br>

<b>Euler triangle</b> = T(2(b<sup>4</sup> + c<sup>4</sup> -
b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>a<sup>2</sup> -
a<sup>2</sup>b<sup>2</sup>), (b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> -
a<sup>2</sup>)(a<sup>2</sup> + b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>))<br>

<b>2nd Euler triangle</b> = T(2a<sup>2</sup>(b<sup>4</sup> +
c<sup>4</sup> - 2b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>a<sup>2</sup>
- a<sup>2</sup>b<sup>2</sup>), (a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup> +
c<sup>2</sup>)(a<sup>4</sup> + b<sup>4</sup> + c<sup>4</sup> -
2a<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup> - 2b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup>))<br>

<b>3rd Euler triangle</b> = T((b - c)<sup>2</sup>, c<sup>2</sup> -
a<sup>2</sup> - bc)<br>

<b>4th Euler triangle</b> = T((b + c)<sup>2</sup>, c<sup>2</sup> -
a<sup>2</sup> + bc)<br>

<b>5th Euler triangle</b> = T(2(b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup>),
b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> - a<sup>2</sup> )
</p>
<p>

The vertices of the five Euler triangles lie on the nine-point circle.  (See C. Kimberling, "Twenty-one points on the nine-point circle," <I>Mathematical Gazette</I> 92 (2008) 29-38.)  Randy Hutson observes (January 8, 2015) that the <b>2nd Euler triangle</B> is the complement of the circumorthic triangle, the <b>2nd Euler triangle</B> is the reflection of the orthic triangle in X(5); the <b>3rd Euler triangle</B> is the complement of the 1st circumperp triangle, the <b>4th Euler triangle</B> is the complement of the 2nd circumperp triangle; the <b>4th Euler triangle</B> the triangle whose sidelines are the radical axes of each of the Odehnal tritangent circles (defined at X(6176)) and the corresponding excircle; the <b>5th Euler triangle</B> is the complement of the circummedial triangle, and the vertices of the <b>5th Euler triangle</B> are the intersections, other than midpoints of the sides of ABC, of the nine-point circle and the medians.

</p>

<p>The notation T(f(a,b,c),g(b,c,a)) can be used to define several more
triangles; in each case, two of the perspectivities can be used to
construct the triangle.</p>

<p><b>T(bc, b<sup>2</sup>)</b> is perspective to ABC with perspector
X(6); other such pairs: incentral, X(192), excentral, X(1045);
tangential, X(6); anticomplementary, X(192)</p>

<p><b>T(-bc, b<sup>2</sup>)</b> is perspective to ABC with perspector
X(6); incentral, X(2); tangential, X(6); T(bc,b<sup>2</sup>), X(6)</p>

<p><b>T(bc, c<sup>2</sup>)</b> is perspective to ABC with perspector
X(76); anticomplementary, X(8); 4th Brocard, X(76), Fuhrmann, X(8);
cevian(X(350)),X(1)</p>

<p><b>T(-bc, c<sup>2</sup>)</b> is perspective to ABC with perspector
X(76); cevian(X(321)),X(75); anticevian(X(8)), X(2)</p>

<p><b>T(a<sup>2</sup>, a<sup>2</sup> - b<sup>2</sup>)</b> is
perspective to the medial triangle with perspector X(3); tangential ,
X(3); symmedial, X(2); cevian(X(287)), X(98)</p>

<p><b>T(a<sup>2</sup>, a<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>)</b> is
perspective to the tangential triangle with perspector X(25); orthic ,
X(25); medial, X(6); 1st Lemoine, X(1383); circummedial, X(251)</p>

<p><b>T(a<sup>2</sup>, a<sup>2</sup> + b<sup>2</sup>)</b> is
perspective to ABC with perspector X(83); medial, X(6); 1st Neuberg,
X(182)</p>

<p><b>T(a<sup>2</sup>, a<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup>)</b> is
perspective to ABC with perspector X(141); medial, X(3); 1st Neuberg,
X(182); T(a<sup>2</sup>,a<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>), X(2);
cevian(X(69)), X(6); cevian(X(76)), X(2); cevian(X(3313)),X(39)</p>

<p>The triangles IT1 and IT2 are triply perspective to ABC (as are T1
and T3). Order-label IT1 as A'B'C'. The three perspectivities are then
given by</p>

<p>AA'&cap;BB'&cap;CC' = X(3862)<br>
AB'&cap;BC'&cap;CA' = f(a,b,c) : f(b,c,a) : f(c,a,b), where f(a,b,c) =
ab(a<sup>2</sup> - bc)(c<sup>2</sup> - ab)(b<sup>2</sup> + bc +
c<sup>2</sup>)<br>
AC'&cap;BA'&cap;CB' = f(a,c,b) : f(b,a,c) : f(c,b,a)</p>

<p>Peter Moses noted (12/23/2011) that T(bc, b<sup>2</sup>) is
<i>triply perspective</i> to ABC. With T(bc, b<sup>2</sup>)
order-labeled as A'B'C', the three perspectivities with perspectors are
as follows:</p>

<p>AA'&cap;BB'&cap;CC' = X(6); AB'&cap;BC'&cap;CA' = P(6);
AC'&cap;BA'&cap;CB' = U(6). (The notation P(k) and U(k) refers to a
bicentric pair of points; see <b>Tables</b> at the top of ETC.)<br>
Likewise, T(bc, c<sup>2</sup>) is triply perspective to ABC, with
perspectors: X(76), P(10), and U(10).</p>
