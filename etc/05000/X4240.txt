<h3 id="X4240">X(4240)&nbsp;=&nbsp;DAO TWELVE EULER LINES POINT</h3>
Barycentrics&nbsp;&nbsp; f(a,b,c) : f(b,c,a) : f(c,a,b), where f(a,b,c) = (b<sup>4</sup> + c<sup>4</sup> - 2a<sup>4</sup> + a<sup>2</sup>b<sup>2</sup> + a<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup> - 2b<sup>2</sup>c<sup>2</sup>)/[(b<sup>2</sup> - c<sup>2</sup>)(b<sup>2</sup> + c<sup>2</sup> - a<sup>2</sup>)]
<br>
<b>
X(4240) = 3X(2) - 4X(402) = 3X(2) - 2X(1650) = X(2) + 2X(3081) = 2X(402) - 3X(1651) = 2X(402) + 3X(3081) = X(1650) - 3X(1651) = X(1650) + 3X(3081) &nbsp;&nbsp; (Peter Moses, April 3, 2013)  </b>
<p>
As a point on the Euler line, X(4240) has Shinagawa coefficients ((E - 8F)F,6(E + F)F - 2S<sup>2</sup>).
</p>
<p>
X(4240) is the point of intersection of the Euler lines of twelve triangles, constructed as in the next few sentences.
</p>
<p>
Let E be the Euler line of a triangle ABC. Let A<sub>1</sub> =
E&cap;BC, and define B<sub>1</sub> and C<sub>1</sub> cyclically. Let
A<sub>B</sub> be the reflection of A in B<sub>1</sub>, and define
B<sub>C</sub> and C<sub>A</sub> cyclically. Let A<sub>C</sub> be the
reflection of C in B<sub>1</sub>, and define B<sub>A</sub> and
C<sub>B</sub> cyclically. The Euler lines of the four triangles ABC,
AA<sub>B</sub>A<sub>C</sub>, BB<sub>C</sub>B<sub>A</sub>,
CC<sub>A</sub>C<sub>B</sub> concur in X(4240). (Dao Thanh Oai, Problem
1 in attachment to ADGEOM #1709, September 15, 2014). See also Telv
Cohl, 'Dao's Theorem on the Concurrence of Three Euler Lines,'
<i>International Journal of Geometry</i> 3 (2014) 70-73: <a href=
"http://ijgeometry.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/8.pdf" style=
"font-weight: bold">Dao's Theorem</a>.
</p>
<p>
Continuing, let A*B*C* be the paralogic triangle of ABC whose
perspectrix is E. Then X(4240) lies on the Euler line of A*B*C*. (Dao
Thanh Oai, noted just after Figure 1 in attachment to ADGEOM #1709,
September 15, 2014).
</p>
<p>
Continuing, redefine A<sub>B</sub> as the point on line AC and
A<sub>C</sub> as the point on line AB such that B<sub>1</sub>,
A<sub>1</sub>, A<sub>B</sub>, A<sub>C</sub> line on a circle and
A<sub>1</sub>, A<sub>B</sub>, A<sub>C</sub> are collinear. Define
B<sub>C</sub> and B<sub>A</sub> cyclically, and define C<sub>A</sub>
and C<sub>B</sub> cyclically. Let A<sub>2</sub> =
B<sub>A</sub>B<sub>C</sub>&cap;C<sub>A</sub>C<sub>B</sub> and define
B<sub>2</sub> and C<sub>2</sub> cyclically. The Euler lines of the five
triangles ABC, A<sub>2</sub>B<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>,
AA<sub>B</sub>A<sub>C</sub>, BB<sub>C</sub>B<sub>A</sub>,
CC<sub>A</sub>C<sub>B</sub> concur in X(4240). (Dao Thanh Oai, Problem
2 in attachment to ADGEOM #1709, September 15, 2014).
</p>
<p>
Continuing, The Euler lines of the triangles
A<sub>2</sub>B<sub>A</sub>C<sub>A</sub>,
B<sub>2</sub>C<sub>B</sub>A<sub>B</sub>,
C<sub>2</sub>A<sub>C</sub>B<sub>C</sub> concur in X(4240). (Dao Thanh
Oai, September 17, 2014).
</p>
<p>
X(4240) is the only point on the Euler line whose trilinear polar is parallel to the Euler line.  (Randy Hutson, January 29, 2015)
</p>
<p>
X(4240) is one of three points used to define a 10-point circle, denoted by D at X(7740).  (Dao Thanh Oai, June 24, 2015)
</p>
<p>
The Gossard triangle is homothetic to the anticomplementary triangle, and the homothetic center is X(4240).  See
Sava Grozdev and Deko Dekov, <i>International Journal of Computer Discovered Mathematics,</i>
<a href="http://www.journal-1.eu/2016-2/Grozdev-Dekov-Gossard-Triangle-pp.104-108.pdf
"> <b>Computer Discovered Mathematics: A Note on the Gossard Triangle.</b></a>
</p>
